📖 The story

Same skills, different lens. The story here is that each company has its own language for ‘great leader’, and sounding generic is what gets you a polite no.

The technical bar is shared — that lives in the pillars. What changes per company is emphasis and vocabulary: Google’s attributes, Microsoft’s Model-Coach-Care, Amazon’s Leadership Principles, and so on.

So you do one high-leverage thing before each loop: take your two strongest stories and re-skin them in that company’s words. Then you stop sounding like a candidate and start sounding like one of them.

How to use this

Pick your target, read its section, then do one thing: pre-map two of your strongest stories onto that company's top values. In the room, you'll reach for the right vocabulary automatically. The technical bar (system design, delivery, security) is shared across all of them — that lives in the pillars; this page is about framing and emphasis.

A universal director loop

Most big-tech leadership loops include: a system/architecture round, a people & delivery management round, a technical/strategy round, one or two behavioral rounds against a values framework, and often a hiring manager + cross-functional conversation. Prepare the shared skills once; tailor the behavioral framing per company.

G GoogleGoogleyness & Leadership · General Cognitive Ability
⭐ Full guide

Google is the priority focus of this site — see the dedicated Google Prep page for the complete deep-dive and question bank. This is the short version.

What they test

Google evaluates four broad areas, even for leaders: General Cognitive Ability (how you structure ambiguous problems), Role-Related Knowledge (system design, technical depth), Leadership (emergent leadership, influence), and Googleyness (collaboration, comfort with ambiguity, doing the right thing). Decisions are made by a hiring committee from written feedback — so being clear and structured matters as much as being right.

How to win

  • Think out loud and structure everything — state assumptions, lay out options, then decide. The committee reads how you reason.
  • Be data-driven and intellectually humble; show you change your mind with evidence.
  • Design for scale and ambiguity; comfort with "it depends" and incomplete information is Googley.
  • Emergent leadership: show you step up to lead and step back to let others lead.

Likely questions

  • "Design a system to serve [X] for hundreds of millions of users." (scale, trade-offs)
  • "Tell me about a time you led without formal authority."
  • "How do you make a decision with significant ambiguity and incomplete data?"
  • "Describe a time you changed your mind based on data."
Frame it Google

"Let me structure this. My assumptions are X and Y; the key tension is consistency vs. availability at this scale; here are two options and the trade-offs; I'd start with the reversible one and instrument it so the data tells us if we're wrong."

MS MicrosoftModel · Coach · Care · Growth mindset

What they test

Microsoft leaders are assessed against Model, Coach, Care — do you model excellence, coach your people, and care about them and the broader team? Underpinning everything is the growth mindset: learn-it-all over know-it-all. They value cross-team collaboration ("one Microsoft") and turning failures into learning.

How to win

  • Lead stories with "as a leader, I…" — emphasize how you grew people and enabled teams, not just what you personally built.
  • Show growth mindset: talk openly about a failure and the learning, without defensiveness.
  • Stress collaboration across org boundaries over individual heroics.
  • Demonstrate empathy and inclusion in how you lead.

Likely questions

  • "How do you coach an underperforming engineer?" (Coach)
  • "Tell me about a failure and what you learned." (growth mindset)
  • "How do you collaborate with teams outside your org?" (Care / one Microsoft)
  • "How do you create an inclusive, high-trust team?"
Frame it Microsoft

"As a leader, my first move was to understand the root cause, then coach rather than rescue — I set clear expectations, paired them with a mentor, and we treated the miss as a learning loop. The team came out stronger and more willing to take smart risks."

aws Amazon16 Leadership Principles · Bar Raiser · deeply STAR

What they test

Amazon maps almost every question to its 16 Leadership Principles. Expect a heavily behavioral loop with a Bar Raiser (an objective interviewer who guards the hiring bar). Answers must be specific, data-backed STAR stories. Key LPs for leaders: Customer Obsession, Ownership, Invent and Simplify, Dive Deep, Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit, Deliver Results, Hire and Develop the Best.

How to win

  • Prepare a deep STAR bank with metrics — expect repeated "tell me about a time…" and follow-up drilling ("Dive Deep").
  • Use "I" not "we"; be precise about your specific actions and the data.
  • Show Ownership and long-term thinking; admit what you'd do differently.
  • Have a Backbone story (you disagreed, then committed) and a Customer Obsession story ready.

Likely questions

  • "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager." (Have Backbone)
  • "Describe the most complex problem you dove deep into." (Dive Deep)
  • "A time you sacrificed short-term results for long-term value." (Ownership)
  • "Tell me about a time you raised the hiring bar." (Hire & Develop)
Frame it Amazon

"Situation… my responsibility… I personally did these three things, backed by this data… the result was [metric], and what I'd refine next time is…" — tie it explicitly to the principle the interviewer is probing.

ATL AtlassianValues round · Team Anywhere · play as a team

What they test

Atlassian runs a dedicated Values Interview alongside technical and leadership rounds. Their values: Open company, no bullshit; Build with heart and balance; Don't #@!% the customer; Play as a team; Be the change you seek. As a distributed-first ("Team Anywhere") company, they probe how you lead and collaborate across locations and time zones.

How to win

  • Be candid and self-aware — "open company, no bullshit" rewards honesty about trade-offs and failures.
  • Show genuine customer empathy and team-first behavior over personal glory.
  • Have distributed-leadership stories: async communication, inclusion across time zones, written-first culture.
  • Connect decisions to customer impact explicitly.

Likely questions

  • "Tell me about a time you put the customer first even when it was costly."
  • "How do you lead a distributed team across time zones?"
  • "Describe giving difficult, candid feedback."
  • "A time you championed a change others resisted." (Be the change)
Frame it Atlassian

"I'll be candid about the trade-off we got wrong first… we put the customer impact above hitting our internal date, communicated it openly and async so every region was in the loop, and the team made the call together."

T Target (enterprise / retail tech)Scale & reliability · cost stewardship · partnership

What they test

Enterprise engineering leadership (Target and similar retailers/banks/insurers) emphasizes reliability at scale (peak events like holiday traffic), cost stewardship, modernization of legacy systems, and strong stakeholder partnership with non-technical business units. Culture often centers on care for people and inclusive, values-led leadership.

How to win

  • Tell reliability/scale stories — capacity planning, DR, handling peak traffic without melting down.
  • Show cost-consciousness and ROI thinking; you steward a budget, not just ship features.
  • Demonstrate partnership with business stakeholders and translating tech into business value.
  • Have a modernization story: migrating or de-risking legacy systems while keeping the lights on.

Likely questions

  • "How did you prepare a system for a major peak event?"
  • "Tell me about modernizing a legacy platform without disrupting the business."
  • "How do you balance delivery speed with cost and reliability?"
  • "Describe partnering with a difficult business stakeholder."
Frame it enterprise

"Heading into peak, I derived our RTO/RPO from the cost of downtime, load-tested to 3× expected traffic, and set an error budget the whole org agreed to — we held availability through the event and came in under the infra budget."

ok Okta (security / identity)Zero Trust · secure-by-default · customer trust

What they test

Security-first companies (Okta and similar) expect leaders who treat security and customer trust as foundational. Deep comfort with identity, authentication/authorization, Zero Trust, and incident maturity is assumed. They probe how you build a secure-by-default culture without grinding delivery to a halt.

How to win

  • Be fluent in identity: AuthN vs AuthZ, OAuth/OIDC, MFA, Zero Trust, least privilege (see Security).
  • Show "guardrails not gates" — security that enables velocity via paved roads and shift-left automation.
  • Have a credible incident-response story: calm containment, blameless post-mortem, systemic fix.
  • Frame everything around customer trust as a product feature.

Likely questions

  • "How do you build security into the SDLC without slowing teams?"
  • "Walk me through how you'd handle a credential leak / security incident."
  • "Explain Zero Trust and how you'd roll it out."
  • "How do you balance security requirements with delivery pressure?"
Frame it security-first

"I make the secure path the easy path — secret-scanning in CI, secure defaults, and shift-left checks — so trust is built in rather than bolted on. When an incident hits, I optimize for fast, calm containment and a blameless fix of the system that allowed it."

V Visa (payments / fintech)Scale & reliability · security/PCI · trust at planet scale

What they test

Payments leadership is about reliability, security, and scale where mistakes move real money. Expect deep system-design on high-throughput, low-latency, highly-available transaction processing; strong security and compliance grounding (PCI DSS, fraud, encryption); and leadership/behavioral rounds around operating mission-critical systems and partnering across a large global organization. Visa emphasizes values such as being bold, decisive, collaborative, and outcome-oriented — verify the current wording on their careers site.

How to win

  • Have crisp stories on availability and resilience — near-zero RTO/RPO, active-active, designing for failure (see System Design).
  • Show security and compliance maturity without treating it as a blocker — secure-by-default, least privilege, incident response (see Security).
  • Demonstrate stewardship of mission-critical systems: change management, blast-radius thinking, careful rollouts.
  • Frame impact in terms of trust, correctness, and customer/partner outcomes, not just velocity.

Likely questions

  • "Design a system to process millions of transactions per second with strong consistency."
  • "How do you guarantee no double-charges under retries and failures?" (idempotency)
  • "Walk me through preparing a payments system for a peak event."
  • "Tell me about leading through a high-severity production incident."
Frame it Visa

"At payments scale I optimize for correctness and availability first — idempotent writes so retries never double-charge, active-active across regions for near-zero RTO/RPO, and change management that keeps the blast radius tiny. Security and trust aren't a gate at the end; they're designed in from the start."

The shared bar

Whatever the logo, director loops converge on a few things. Nail these and you're ready for most:

  • Scope & impact: you operate at the level of teams, systems, and business outcomes — not tasks.
  • Judgment under ambiguity: structured thinking, naming trade-offs, reversible vs one-way-door decisions.
  • People leadership: hiring, growing, coaching, handling underperformance, building psychological safety.
  • Delivery: predictable shipping, DORA metrics, incident handling, tech-debt management.
  • Communication: translating engineering into business value for executives, concisely.
  • Authenticity: real stories, owned failures, and self-awareness beat polished but hollow answers.
Universal tip

Prepare the shared skills once on the pillar pages, build a strong STAR story bank, then spend the last day re-skinning your top stories in each target company's vocabulary. That's the highest-leverage prep there is.

Keep it current

Company values, frameworks, and interview formats evolve. Treat these as well-established patterns, but verify the latest published values and interview guidance on each company's careers site before your loop.
