📖 The story

‘Run this job at this time’ sounds simple until a worker crashes mid-run, or two workers grab the same job. The whole design is about running each job — despite failures — exactly enough.

So jobs carry a nextRunAt, workers claim due jobs with a lease/lock so only one runs it, execution is idempotent so retries are safe, and failures go to a retry/dead-letter path.

How to think about it — step by step

State the two fears

A job runs twice, or a job never runs. Why: the whole design defends against these.

Track due times

Store each job with a 'next run at' and poll for due ones. Why: simple, and easy to partition.

Prevent double runs

A worker claims a job with a lease/lock that auto-expires if it crashes. Why: only one owner runs it, and a dead worker releases it.

Make jobs idempotent

Design tasks to be safe to run twice. Why: with at-least-once execution, retries will happen.

Name the trade-off

At-least-once + idempotency (simple, safe) vs exactly-once (hard). Why: almost always choose the former.

1 · Requirements

Functional: schedule one-off and recurring jobs; run them near their due time; survive failures. Non-functional: no duplicate runs, no missed jobs, scalable to millions of jobs.

2 · Architecture

📍 Real example — cron expressions

A cron expression is five fields: minute hour day-of-month month day-of-week. A few real ones:

0 2 * * 1       →  02:00 every Monday
*/15 * * * *    →  every 15 minutes
0 0 1 * *       →  midnight on the 1st of each month
30 9 * * 1-5    →  09:30 Monday–Friday
0 */6 * * *     →  every 6 hours (00:00, 06:00, 12:00, 18:00)

What this teaches: * means “every”, */n means “every n”, and ranges like 1-5 work. Two leadership gotchas: cron has no timezone of its own (decide UTC vs local, or daylight-saving will surprise you), and thousands of jobs set to 0 0 * * * all fire at once — a thundering herd — so add a little random jitter to spread them out.

3 · The hard parts

  • No double-run: a lease/lock (DB row lock, or Redis with TTL) ensures a single owner; the lease auto-expires if the worker dies.
  • Idempotency: jobs must be safe to run twice (at-least-once), since failures cause retries.
  • Timeliness: short poll intervals or a time-wheel; partition so the due-time scan stays cheap.

4 · Trade-offs

  • At-least-once + idempotency (simple, safe) vs exactly-once (hard).
  • Polling interval: tighter = timelier but more load.

Key sub-systems

  • Job store — jobs + nextRunAt, partitioned.
  • Dispatcher — polls due jobs.
  • Lease/lock — single-owner claim.
  • Workers — idempotent execution.
  • Retry/DLQ — backoff on failure.

Bottlenecks & what to optimize next

  • The due-time scan can bottleneck — partition jobs and index by nextRunAt.
  • A thundering herd at a popular cron minute — jitter schedules.

Common follow-up questions

How do you prevent a job running twice?

A worker claims a job with a lease/lock that auto-expires on crash; only the owner runs it.

What if a worker dies mid-job?

The lease expires and another worker re-claims it; because jobs are idempotent, re-running is safe.

How do you keep jobs timely?

Short poll intervals or a time-wheel, plus partitioning so the due-time scan stays cheap.

🧠 Remember it

Job scheduler = due-time poll + lease to avoid double-runs + idempotency.

  • nextRunAt + partitioned store; dispatcher polls due jobs.
  • Lease/lock = single owner; auto-expire on crash.
  • Idempotent execution; retries + DLQ.
