System Design

You won't be asked to draw boxes for an hour — you'll be asked to reason about scale, cost, and failure like someone who's accountable when the system goes down at 2 a.m. Here's the vocabulary and a repeatable way to answer.

A 6-step framework for any design question

When asked "how would you design X," resist jumping to a solution. Walk this path out loud — it shows structured thinking, which matters more than any specific component choice.

Say it like a director

"Before I design anything, I want to understand the load we're solving for and what 'good' means here — is this 1,000 users or 100 million, and are we optimizing for cost, latency, or reliability? The right architecture for a startup MVP is the wrong one for a regulated bank, so let me anchor on the constraints first."

Vertical scaling — "scale up"

Make a single machine more powerful: add CPU, RAM, faster disks. It's the simplest path because the application usually doesn't change. But there's a ceiling — you can only buy so big a box — and that box is a single point of failure.

✅ Strengths

Simple, no code changes, no distributed-systems complexity, great for databases that are hard to shard, lowest operational overhead to start.

⚠ Limits

Hard hardware ceiling, expensive at the top end, single point of failure, usually needs downtime to resize, doesn't improve fault tolerance.

Horizontal scaling — "scale out"

Add more machines and spread the work across them. This is how internet-scale systems grow. It removes the single-machine ceiling and improves fault tolerance — if one node dies, others carry on — but it requires the application to be stateless and introduces a load balancer and distributed-systems concerns.

Say it like a director

"In practice it's not either/or. I'd scale the database vertically first because it's simpler and many DBs don't shard cleanly, and scale the stateless app tier horizontally behind a load balancer. The architecture goal is that growth becomes a budgeting decision — add nodes — rather than a re-architecture project."

Load balancing

A load balancer (LB) is the traffic cop that distributes incoming requests across your servers so no single one is overwhelmed. It also does health checks — if a server stops responding, the LB stops sending it traffic. It's the component that makes horizontal scaling and zero-downtime deploys possible.

  • Round robin — send each request to the next server in turn. Simple, good when servers are equal.
  • Least connections — send to the server with the fewest active requests. Better for uneven workloads.
  • IP hash / sticky sessions — same client goes to the same server. Needed when state isn't externalized (avoid if you can — prefer stateless).
  • Health checks — automatically route around unhealthy nodes; the foundation of self-healing systems.

Caching

A cache stores the results of expensive work close to where it's needed so you don't redo it. It's the highest-leverage performance tool you have — and the source of some of the nastiest bugs ("there are only two hard problems in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things").

Where to cache

Browser, CDN (edge), application memory, a dedicated cache (Redis/Memcached), and the database's own buffer. Each layer is closer to the user and faster.

The hard part

Invalidation — keeping cached data fresh. Strategies: TTL (expire after N seconds), write-through, and explicit eviction on change. Stale cache = wrong answers.

Watch out for

Cache stampede (many misses at once hammering the DB), and over-caching personalized or sensitive data. Cache the public and the hot, not the private.

Business Continuity Planning (BCP)

BCP is the broad plan for keeping the business running through any disruption — not just IT outages, but also loss of an office, a key vendor, or key people. It answers: "if something bad happens, how do we keep serving customers and making money?" Disaster recovery (next) is the technology subset of BCP.

BCP vs. DR in one line

BCP = keep the whole business operating. DR = restore the IT systems and data. DR lives inside BCP.

  • Business Impact Analysis (BIA) — which processes are critical, and what does an hour of downtime cost?
  • Risk assessment — what could disrupt us (outage, region failure, cyber-attack, supplier loss)?
  • Continuity strategies — redundancy, failover, manual workarounds, alternate suppliers.
  • Communication plan — who decides, who tells customers, who tells regulators.
  • Testing — a plan you've never rehearsed is a guess. Run game days and tabletop exercises.

Disaster Recovery (DR)

DR is how you bring systems and data back after a major failure — a region outage, data corruption, ransomware. The art is matching the strategy (and its cost) to how much downtime and data loss the business can actually tolerate.

RTO & RPO — the two numbers that drive DR

These are the questions an interviewer is really probing when they ask about disaster recovery. Know them cold.

RPO — Recovery Point Objective

The maximum data loss you can accept, measured in time. RPO of 5 minutes means you must back up / replicate at least every 5 minutes. Tighter RPO → continuous replication → more cost.

RTO — Recovery Time Objective

The maximum acceptable downtime before service is restored. RTO of 1 hour means everything — detect, decide, fail over, validate — must finish within an hour. Tighter RTO → hotter standby → more cost.

Say it like a director

"DR design starts with two business questions, not a technology choice: how much data can we afford to lose — that's our RPO — and how long can we be down — that's our RTO. For the payments system both are near zero, so I'd justify active-active across regions. For an internal reporting tool, a nightly backup with a few hours' RTO is responsible stewardship of budget. I'd never apply one DR posture to everything."

Classic trap

If asked "how do you guarantee zero downtime and zero data loss," don't say "we replicate everything." Say: "Near-zero is achievable with active-active multi-region, but it roughly doubles infrastructure cost and adds consistency complexity. I'd confirm the business truly needs it for this system before committing — otherwise it's an expensive solution to a problem we don't have."

Practice questions

When would you scale vertically vs. horizontally?Core

Vertical first when it's simple and sufficient — especially for relational databases that don't shard cleanly, or when we just need a quick win and aren't near the hardware ceiling. Horizontal once we need fault tolerance, are approaching that ceiling, or want growth to be a matter of adding commodity nodes rather than a re-architecture. In a typical system I'd run the stateless app tier horizontally behind a load balancer and scale the database vertically until sharding or read replicas are genuinely warranted. The trade-off I'd name is simplicity and cost vs. resilience and ceiling.

Our database is the bottleneck under load. Walk me through your options.Deep

I'd go cheapest-and-safest first. (1) Measure — find the actual slow queries; often it's a missing index, not a capacity problem. (2) Cache hot reads in Redis to take pressure off the DB. (3) Read replicas to offload read traffic. (4) Scale vertically for a bigger box. (5) Only then shard / partition, because that adds real complexity. Throughout, I'd be asking whether the read/write ratio or data model is the real issue. The leadership point: I escalate complexity deliberately, and I don't shard a database to avoid adding an index.

What's the difference between BCP and DR?Core

Business continuity is the whole-business plan for surviving any disruption — people, offices, suppliers, and systems — so we keep serving customers. Disaster recovery is the IT subset: restoring systems and data after a major failure. DR lives inside BCP. The reason I keep them distinct is that a perfect DR plan still fails the business if nobody knows who communicates with customers or which manual workaround keeps orders flowing while systems come back. Continuity is bigger than the tech.

Define RTO and RPO and how they drive architecture.Core

RPO is how much data we can afford to lose, in time — it sets our backup/replication frequency. RTO is how long we can be down before restored — it sets how "hot" our standby needs to be. They're business decisions that translate directly into cost: a near-zero RPO/RTO points to active-active multi-region with continuous replication; a relaxed one points to periodic backups. I always derive the architecture from these two numbers rather than picking a fashionable DR pattern and hoping it fits.

Design a URL shortener at a high level. (Keep it leadership-level.)Deep

I'd clarify scale first — reads vastly outnumber writes, so this is a read-heavy, cache-friendly problem. High level: an API to create short codes, a key-value store mapping code → long URL, a cache (or CDN) in front for the hot redirects, and a load-balanced stateless app tier. Codes can be generated from a counter encoded in base-62 or a hash with collision checks. Because it's read-heavy and the data is small and immutable, caching gives us huge leverage and the DB choice is almost secondary. I'd flag analytics (click counts) as an async, eventually-consistent write so it never slows the redirect. I'm keeping this at the component-and-trade-off level rather than coding it — that's the altitude I operate at.

The site is slow but CPU and memory look fine. How do you lead the investigation?Trap

"Looks fine" on averages often hides a tail problem, so first I'd look at p95/p99 latency, not averages, and at the dependencies — a slow downstream call, database lock contention, an exhausted connection pool, or a noisy neighbor. I'd have the team trace a slow request end-to-end rather than guess. As a leader my job is to prevent thrashing: insist on data before changes, one hypothesis at a time, and a clear owner for the incident. The trap here is throwing more hardware at a problem that's actually a blocked thread pool or a missing index — that wastes money and hides the real cause.
