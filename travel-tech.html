📖 The story

A TMC (Travel Management Company) is a B2B business: it manages travel for companies — booking flights, hotels, rail and cars within policy, looking after travellers, and reporting the spend. FCM is the corporate TMC brand of Flight Centre Travel Group (FCTG).

Underneath, the hard part is content: airfares and rules historically flow through Global Distribution Systems (GDS) using a terse 1980s message format (EDIFACT). The industry is shifting to NDC — modern XML offers-and-orders straight from airlines — and a TMC platform has to blend old and new into one clean booking experience, then fulfil, service, and report on it.

The TMC workflow — shop to settle

What these actually look like

PNR, EDIFACT and NDC get thrown around abstractly. Here’s a (simplified, illustrative) look at each so the shapes are concrete.

📍 A PNR — the classic GDS booking record

A PNR is identified by a 6-character record locator (e.g. ABC123). In a GDS like Amadeus/Sabre an agent sees a terse, coded display:

--- RLOC: ABC123 ---
1.1 SHARMA/KRISHMA MS
 1  AI 302 Y 15SEP DEL BOM HK1  0805  1015   * flight, class Y, confirmed
 2  AI 309 Y 22SEP BOM DEL HK1  1840  2055   * return segment
TKT/TL  TAW/17SEP                            * ticketing time limit
SSR VGML AI HK1/S2                           * special service: veg meal, segment 2
APE krishma@example.com                      * contact email
RECEIVED FROM - KRISHMA

Each line is a coded element: passenger, flight segments, ticketing limit, special-service requests (SSR), contacts. It’s compact and powerful — but rigid.

📍 EDIFACT — the wire format behind the scenes

When systems talk, that PNR moves as EDIFACT: terse segments where + separates data elements, : separates components, and ' ends a segment.

UNB+IATA:1+1ASIWXYZ+1ASIWXYZ+250915:0805+0001'  interchange header
UNH+0001+PAORES:13:1:1A'                         message: process PNR
ORG+1A+DELAI2100:DEL'                            originating office (Delhi)
SSR+++VGML:HK:1:::S2'                            veg meal, confirmed, segment 2
TIF+SHARMA:KRISHMA:MS'                           traveller name
UNT+15+0001'                                     message trailer (15 segments)
UNZ+1+0001'                                      interchange trailer

Decades-old, extremely compact, machine-efficient — but it can’t easily express rich content like bundles, photos, or personalised offers. That’s the limitation NDC addresses.

📍 NDC — the same booking as modern XML offers & orders

NDC is XML and models an Offer (what the airline proposes) that becomes an Order (what you bought) — with ancillaries as first-class items:

<Offer OfferID="OFFER-AI-7Y3K" Owner="AI">
  <TotalPrice><SimpleCurrencyPrice Code="INR">7450</SimpleCurrencyPrice></TotalPrice>
  <OfferItem OfferItemID="OI-1">
    <Service><ServiceID>FLIGHT-DEL-BOM-15SEP</ServiceID></Service>   the flight
  </OfferItem>
  <OfferItem OfferItemID="OI-2">
    <Service><ServiceID>BAG-20KG</ServiceID></Service>            ancillary: extra bag
  </OfferItem>
</Offer>

// after the customer accepts, it becomes an Order:
<Order OrderID="ORD-AI-9F2Q1K" Owner="AI">
  <TotalPrice><SimpleCurrencyPrice Code="INR">7450</SimpleCurrencyPrice></TotalPrice>
  <OrderItem>… flight + bag …</OrderItem>
</Order>

Verbose vs EDIFACT, but expressive: branded fares, seats, bags, photos and personalised pricing all fit naturally.

🔑 The same trip, three identifiers
GDS / PNRrecord locator → ABC123 E-ticket13-digit number → 098 2412345678 (098 = airline prefix) NDCOrder ID → ORD-AI-9F2Q1K (no separate ticket; the Order is the record)

The shift NDC-X is driving: from “PNR + e-ticket” to a single Order as the system of record.

📍 Real example — caching GDS availability (and why TTLs are short)

Calling the GDS (Amadeus/Sabre/Travelport) for live fares is slow and metered per transaction. So a TMC caches results — but flight prices and seats change by the minute, so the TTL (time-to-live) must be short and the booking must be re-validated:

search LHR→JFK, 12 Aug
  cache MISS → call GDS (Fare Quote) ~1200 ms
  store: key="LHR-JFK-2026-08-12-1ADT"  value=offers  TTL=120s

same search 40s later
  cache HIT → return in ~5 ms   (no GDS call, no GDS transaction fee)

user clicks BOOK at 200s (cache already expired)
  → do NOT trust cached price — re-price + confirm availability live, THEN create the PNR
  → if fare changed: show "price updated" before payment

What this teaches: caching shopping results cuts latency from ~1s to ~5ms and slashes GDS transaction costs, but a stale price is a real liability — the airline owns the fare and can change it. So you keep the search cache short (often 60–300s) and always re-validate price and availability at the moment of booking before you commit the PNR and take payment. This “cache to shop, verify to book” pattern is core to every travel platform.

Here is what those travel formats look like, in plain terms. A PNR, or passenger name record, is the booking file: it holds a six-character locator such as A-B-C-1-2-3, the passenger name, and the flight segments. An EDIFACT message is the older telex-style format — a series of coded segments like U-N-B, U-N-H, and S-S-R. NDC is the modern XML format, where the airline sends an Offer and you create an Order, more like shopping on a website and with far richer content than the old segment codes.

What is a TMC, and how does FCM/FCTG fit in?Core

A TMC (Travel Management Company) manages business travel on behalf of corporate clients — booking flights, hotels, rail and cars within the company’s travel policy, providing 24/7 traveller support and duty of care, negotiating fares, and reporting spend back to the client. It’s a B2B business (the customer is the company, not the individual traveller). FCM is the corporate-travel TMC brand of Flight Centre Travel Group (FCTG); its technology is delivered as SaaS platforms used by corporate bookers and travel consultants.

What is a GDS, and which ones matter?Core

A GDS (Global Distribution System) is the aggregator that historically connects travel sellers (TMCs, agencies, online booking tools) to airline, hotel, car and rail inventory and fares. The big three are Amadeus, Sabre, and Travelport. For decades the GDS has been the central marketplace: a TMC searches and books across many airlines through one GDS connection rather than integrating each airline directly.

What is EDIFACT, and why is it still around?Deep

EDIFACT (UN/EDIFACT) is the terse, structured electronic-messaging standard the airline/GDS world has used since the 1980s for availability, pricing, booking and ticketing messages. It’s compact and battle-tested but rigid — it carries fares and a PNR well, but struggles to express rich, modern content like branded fares, bundles, seat maps and personalised offers. It persists because the entire distribution chain runs on it; replacing it is a multi-year industry migration, which is exactly what NDC is driving.

What is NDC, and what problem does it solve?Deep

NDC (New Distribution Capability) is IATA’s XML-based standard that lets airlines distribute rich, dynamic content — branded fares, ancillaries (bags, seats, wifi), bundles and personalised offers — directly to sellers, instead of being flattened into EDIFACT/GDS fares. It moves the model from “fares in a GDS” to an Offer & Order model: the airline creates an Offer, the seller turns it into an Order. For a TMC it means richer content and merchandising, but also more integrations and complexity to manage.

EDIFACT vs NDC — how does a TMC platform handle both?Deep

You build an aggregation/normalisation layer that hides the source. The platform shops EDIFACT (via GDS), NDC (via airline APIs or an aggregator), and direct/LCC connects in parallel, then normalises everything into one internal offer model so bookers see a single, comparable list. The hard parts are reconciling content and servicing differences (an NDC order and a GDS PNR are serviced differently — changes, refunds, exchanges), and keeping a consistent record. The leadership framing: abstract the source so the rest of the platform — and the user — doesn’t care whether an offer came from EDIFACT or NDC.

Say it like a director

“I’d treat content sources as pluggable adapters behind a normalised offer/order model. The booking, fulfilment, servicing and reporting layers speak our internal model, so adding NDC airlines or retiring EDIFACT becomes an adapter change, not a platform rewrite.”

What is NDC-X?Deep

NDC-X is IATA’s program to accelerate NDC adoption and drive the industry toward a full Offers and Orders world — ultimately retiring the legacy PNR/EDIFACT and ticket constructs in favour of a single modern order record across the journey. Think of NDC as the standard and NDC-X as the umbrella transformation program (capability building, adoption targets, and the “Offer/Order” end-state). For a TMC leader it signals direction of travel: invest in an order-centric architecture, because the industry is moving there. (Program specifics and timelines evolve — verify current IATA guidance.)

What is a PNR, and how does it differ from an Order?Core

A PNR (Passenger Name Record) is the traditional booking record in the GDS/airline reservation system — itinerary, passenger, contact, ticketing details. In the NDC world, the equivalent is an Order — a single record holding the offer the customer accepted (flights + ancillaries + price), managed via Order Management. The shift from PNR+ticket to a unified Order is the structural change NDC-X is pushing; a modern TMC platform increasingly models bookings as orders regardless of source.

Walk me through the end-to-end TMC booking workflow.Deep

Shop: a traveller/booker searches via an OBT (online booking tool) or a consultant; the platform pulls offers from GDS + NDC + direct and applies travel policy (allowed fares, approvals). Book: the chosen offer becomes a PNR/Order, routed through approval if needed. Ticket / fulfil: the ticket is issued and settled with airlines via BSP/ARC. Mid-office: automated quality control catches errors, applies rules, adds fare savings/robotics. Servicing: 24/7 changes, cancellations, and disruption re-accommodation, with duty-of-care tracking of where travellers are. Back-office: invoicing, reconciliation, and management-information reporting, feeding the client’s expense system.

What is an OBT, and how does it relate to the TMC?Core

An OBT (Online Booking Tool) is the self-service interface corporate travellers use to book within policy (examples in the market include Concur Travel and others, plus TMC-built tools). The TMC sits behind it: providing content, fulfilment, servicing, and reporting, and handling anything the OBT can’t (complex itineraries, after-hours support). A TMC may offer its own OBT as part of its SaaS platform or integrate with third-party OBTs.

What is mid-office vs back-office in a TMC?Deep

Mid-office is the automated quality-control and rules engine between booking and fulfilment: it validates PNRs/orders, enforces policy, fixes common errors, finds fare savings (re-shopping), and queues exceptions for agents — classic “robotics” that scale a small ops team. Back-office handles the money and data after travel: invoicing, reconciliation with BSP/ARC settlement, and MI (management information) reporting on spend, savings, and compliance, often feeding the client’s expense/finance systems. Together they’re where a TMC earns trust on accuracy and savings.

What does BSP/ARC settlement mean?Deep

BSP (Billing and Settlement Plan, IATA — used in most of the world) and ARC (Airlines Reporting Corporation — the US equivalent) are the clearing houses that settle money between travel agencies/TMCs and airlines for tickets issued. Instead of the TMC paying each airline directly, sales are reported and settled centrally on a cycle. It’s the travel-industry analogue of payment settlement — reconciliation against BSP/ARC files is a core back-office function.

How would you architect a modern TMC platform (B2B SaaS)?Deep

Source-agnostic and order-centric. A content/aggregation layer of pluggable adapters (GDS-EDIFACT, NDC, LCC, hotel/rail) normalises into one offer model; a policy & approval engine; an order/PNR service as the system of record; fulfilment (ticketing + BSP/ARC); event-driven mid-office automation; a servicing layer for changes/disruption; and a data/reporting pipeline for MI and expense feeds. Multi-tenant SaaS with per-client policy config, strong duty-of-care (real-time traveller tracking), and resilience (content sources fail often, so degrade gracefully). The director framing: abstract volatile external systems behind adapters, model everything as an order, and treat reliability of third-party content as a first-class design problem.

Why is multi-tenancy and policy configuration central for a TMC SaaS?Deep

A TMC serves many corporate clients on one platform, each with its own travel policy, approval chains, preferred suppliers, negotiated fares, cost centres, and reporting. So the architecture must be multi-tenant with per-client configuration driving behaviour at runtime — without forking the codebase per client. Get this right and onboarding a new corporate is configuration, not engineering; get it wrong and every client becomes a custom build.

Content sources are flaky and slow — how do you design for that?Trap

Treat external content (GDS, NDC airline APIs) as unreliable by default. Search: fan out to sources in parallel, set tight timeouts, stream/aggregate results as they arrive, and cache where freshness allows (fares go stale fast, so short TTLs). Book/ticket: make operations idempotent and use sagas with compensation, because a booking may succeed at the airline but time out to you. Add circuit breakers per source, graceful degradation (show what you have), and strong reconciliation so a half-completed booking is detected and resolved. This mirrors the payments and search deep-dives — the same resilience patterns apply.

How is AI being used in a travel-tech TMC today?Deep

Across the workflow: conversational/agentic booking (book and change trips in natural language); virtual agents for servicing and after-hours support; disruption re-accommodation (automatically finding alternatives when a flight is cancelled); mid-office automation (AI-assisted QC and fare re-shopping); RAG over travel policy and content so answers are grounded in the client’s rules; personalisation and offer ranking; and back-office summarisation/reporting (itinerary summaries, spend insights). The leadership stance is the same as elsewhere: capture the leverage, keep humans on high-stakes actions, ground answers with RAG, and measure with evals.

How would you roll out an AI assistant for corporate travellers?Deep

Start where it’s high-value and low-risk: servicing and Q&A (flight status, “what’s my itinerary”, policy questions) grounded with RAG over the client’s policy and the live booking, before letting it transact. As confidence grows, add agentic actions (rebooking, changes) with guardrails: policy enforcement, spend limits, and human approval on irreversible/expensive steps. Wire it to the order/servicing APIs via tools (MCP-style), keep an audit trail, and gate quality with evals before customer-facing release. Frame it as augmenting consultants — deflecting routine requests so agents handle the complex, high-touch cases.

What's duty of care, and why is it an engineering concern?Core

Duty of care is the employer’s legal and moral obligation to keep travelling employees safe. For a TMC platform that means knowing where every traveller is in real time, alerting on disruptions or risk events (weather, strikes, security), and being able to locate and re-book affected travellers fast. Engineering-wise it’s real-time itinerary tracking, risk-feed integration, and reliable notifications — a core differentiator, not a nice-to-have.

"Let's just connect to one GDS and be done" — your take?Trap

One GDS is the simple start, but it limits content and locks you in. Increasingly, the best fares and rich content (ancillaries, branded fares) come via NDC direct from airlines, and low-cost carriers often aren’t in the GDS at all. So the durable answer is a source-agnostic aggregation layer that can add NDC and direct connects over time without re-plumbing the platform. Betting everything on a single EDIFACT/GDS pipe is a strategic risk as the industry moves to offers-and-orders. Pragmatically: start with the GDS for breadth, but architect for multi-source from day one.

As an engineering leader, how do you navigate the EDIFACT-to-NDC transition?Deep

Treat it as a multi-year migration, not a switch. Build the order-centric, source-agnostic platform now so NDC airlines plug in as adapters; run EDIFACT and NDC side by side and shift content gradually, measuring coverage, look-to-book, and servicing parity (NDC servicing maturity varies by airline). Manage the risk that some carriers’ NDC is immature — keep GDS fallback. Communicate to the business in terms of content richness and cost, and sequence so feature delivery never stalls behind the migration. It’s the same playbook as any big platform migration: abstraction layer, incremental cutover, measure, keep fallback.

What metrics matter for a travel-tech platform?Deep

Look-to-book ratio (searches per booking — high ratios stress and cost you on GDS/NDC calls), search latency and content response/timeout rates per source, booking success / failure and abandonment, NDC vs EDIFACT mix and coverage, online adoption (self-service vs agent), policy compliance and realized savings, and servicing/SLA metrics for disruptions. As a leader I’d tie engineering work to look-to-book cost and booking success, because those move both customer experience and the cost base.

🧠 Remember it

A TMC is B2B SaaS that aggregates volatile travel content and turns it into policy-compliant, serviced, reported orders — increasingly with AI across the workflow.

  • GDS (Amadeus/Sabre/Travelport) + EDIFACT = the legacy rails; NDC (XML, offers & orders) is the modern direction, NDC-X the program driving it.
  • Architect source-agnostic and order-centric: adapters behind a normalised offer/order model, with GDS fallback.
  • Mid-office (QC/robotics) and back-office (BSP/ARC settlement, MI) are where accuracy & savings live; duty of care is real-time and critical.
  • AI: grounded servicing & chat booking first, then guarded agentic actions — augmenting consultants.
