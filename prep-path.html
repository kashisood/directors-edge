The Prep Path

Don't study randomly. Move in three levels of increasing complexity — each builds on the last. Finish a level only when you can say its ideas aloud without notes, then climb. Target: the Google / Microsoft / Amazon director bar.

📖 The story

Random studying feels productive and changes nothing. The story of this path is a climb: you don’t attempt Level 3 cliffs until Level 1 footing is solid.

Level 1 builds the vocabulary and mental models. Level 2 connects them to real decisions — enough to clear a standard director loop. Level 3 is operating in ambiguity and at scale, the FAANG bar.

The gate between levels isn’t reading more — it’s being able to say the ideas aloud, in a leader’s words, without notes. Pass the gate, then climb.

How to use the path

Each pillar page tags its practice questions L1 L2 L3 and gives you a filter at the top of its question bank. Work a level horizontally across all pillars before moving up — breadth first, then depth. Gate yourself: you've cleared a level when you can answer its questions out loud, in the director's vocabulary, without reading.

The gate test

Before climbing a level, record yourself answering three of its questions. If you name the trade-off, give a concrete example, cite a metric, and end on business impact — pass. If you ramble or recite definitions — stay a level.

L1

Foundations

Goal: explain the core concepts clearly · ~1 week

Build the vocabulary and mental models. At this level you should never be caught out by a definition, and you should sound like someone who thinks in trade-offs.

  • Read Fundamentals end to end; master latency vs throughput, CAP, ACID/BASE, stateless, idempotency.
  • Learn the metrics language: SLI/SLO/SLA, p50/p95/p99, error budget, DORA.
  • System design basics: vertical vs horizontal scaling, load balancing, caching (System Design first half).
  • Behavioral basics: the STAR structure and a first draft of 6–8 stories (Behavioral).
  • Skim the Cheat Sheet daily until the terms feel automatic.
  • Drill every pillar's L1 questions using the level filter.

You pass when: you can explain CAP, scaling, and the metric acronyms to a non-engineer in plain language.

L2

Core — interview-ready

Goal: handle a standard director loop · ~2 weeks

Now connect concepts to decisions. This is the level that clears most director interviews: complete system-design reasoning, real leadership judgment, and structured behavioral answers.

  • Full system design: BCP, disaster recovery, RTO/RPO, deployment strategies; do a few end-to-end designs out loud (System Design).
  • Leadership & delivery: team management, project management, release/delivery, DORA, difficult-situation frames (Leadership & Delivery).
  • Security fluency: AuthN vs AuthZ, OAuth/OIDC, secrets & API keys, shift-left (Security).
  • Tech radar: current Java/Spring/AI and a leader's stance on adoption (Tech Radar).
  • Behavioral: polish the story bank, practice the situational skeleton, handle trap questions calmly.
  • Read the frameworks on the Company Prep page for your target companies.
  • Drill every pillar's L2 questions.

You pass when: you can design a system end-to-end naming trade-offs, and tell any story in STAR with a quantified result.

L3

Advanced — the FAANG bar

Goal: crack Google / Microsoft / Amazon-level loops · ~2–3 weeks

Operate in ambiguity and at scale, and communicate like an executive. At this level the interview is less about knowledge and more about judgment, influence, and how you think when there's no clean answer.

  • Design under ambiguity & scale: multi-region, consistency at scale, cost at scale, "design X for 100M users" with explicit assumptions.
  • Org & strategy: org design (Conway's Law), build-vs-buy, platform vs autonomy, multi-year technical strategy, influencing without authority.
  • Executive communication: translate engineering into business outcomes for a VP/exec panel in two sentences.
  • Company tailoring: map your stories to each company's framework (Company Prep) — Googleyness, Microsoft's Model-Coach-Care, Amazon's LPs.
  • Trap & pressure questions: stay composed, question wrong premises with data.
  • Run full mock loops (see below). Drill every pillar's L3 questions.

You pass when: you can lead a design through changing requirements, and reframe any behavioral question to a specific company's values on the fly.

The final week — mock loops

Interviews are a performance; rehearse the performance. In the last week, simulate the real loop rather than reading more.

Daily mock

One 45-minute block per day: pick a random question from each pillar, answer aloud against a timer, and self-grade with the gate test.

One full loop

Back-to-back: a system design, a leadership/delivery deep-dive, a security/tech round, and a behavioral round with a friend playing interviewer.

Company pass

Re-read your target company's section the night before and pre-map two stories to its top values.

Questions for them

Prepare five sharp questions to ask — it's evaluated. See the list on the Behavioral page.

🧠 Remember it

Prep is a climb: L1 footing → L2 decisions → L3 ambiguity — gated by saying it aloud.

  • Work a level across all pillars before climbing (breadth, then depth).
  • The gate test: answer aloud, in director language, no notes.
  • Final week = mock the real loop, not more reading.
