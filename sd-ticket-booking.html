📖 The story

A ticket sale is a thundering herd fighting over a tiny pool of seats, and the unforgivable bug is selling the same seat twice. So the design optimizes for correctness under contention, not raw speed.

Two ideas carry it: a virtual waiting room that throttles the stampede into a steady stream, and an atomic hold-with-TTL on each seat in a strongly-consistent store so exactly one buyer can claim it.

How to think about it — step by step

Name the unforgivable bug

Selling the same seat twice. Why: correctness, not speed, drives this design.

Tame the stampede

A virtual waiting room admits users at a controlled rate. Why: turns a chaotic spike into a steady stream the backend can handle.

Claim seats atomically

Flip a seat free→held with a conditional update (optimistic lock); only one wins. Why: this is what actually prevents double-booking.

Hold, then sell

A hold has a TTL; after payment it becomes sold, else it auto-releases. Why: abandoned carts must free their seats.

Be strict only where needed

Strong consistency on the seat lock; the rest stays highly available. Why: don't pay consistency costs everywhere.

1 · Requirements

Functional: browse events, see available seats, book and pay for specific seats. Non-functional: absolutely no double-booking of a seat, survive enormous demand spikes (a hot show sells out in seconds), and stay fair. This is a contention + consistency problem more than a throughput one.

2 · Capacity

Normal load is light, but a flash sale can bring millions of users in the same minute fighting over thousands of seats — a massive read/write spike concentrated on a tiny hot dataset.

3 · API

  • GET /events/{id}/seats → seat map with availability.
  • POST /holds → reserve seats for a few minutes (returns a holdId + expiry).
  • POST /bookings with holdId + payment (idempotent) → confirm.

4 · Data model

  • seat(eventId, seatId, state {free|held|sold}, holdId, holdExpiry, version) — the version column enables optimistic locking.
  • booking(id, holdId, userId, seatIds, paymentRef, ts).

5 · Architecture

6 · The hard part — no double-booking under a stampede

  • Atomic seat claim: flipping free → held uses a conditional/optimistic update (“set held WHERE state=free AND version=N”); only one request wins, the rest get told the seat’s gone.
  • Holds with TTL: a seat is held while the user pays; if they abandon, the hold expires and the seat returns to the pool.
  • Waiting room: a queue/token system caps how many users hit the booking path at once, turning a chaotic spike into a steady stream.

7 · Scaling & failure

  • The hot data is small (one event’s seats) — keep it in a fast, strongly-consistent store and shard by event.
  • On crash, holds simply expire; idempotent payment prevents double-charge on retry.

8 · Trade-offs

  • Consistency over availability for the seat claim — the one place you must be strict; the rest of the app stays highly available.
  • Waiting room adds user wait but protects correctness and the system.

Key sub-systems

  • Waiting room — queue/token throttles the spike.
  • Booking service — holds + confirmation.
  • Seat inventory — strongly consistent, optimistic locking.
  • Payment — idempotent.
  • Hold expiry — TTL releases abandoned seats.

Bottlenecks & what to optimize next

  • Contention on hot seats is the bottleneck — atomic conditional updates pick one winner.
  • Flash-sale spike — the waiting room caps concurrency into the booking path.
