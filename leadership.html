Leadership & Delivery

This is where directors are really made or broken. The technical questions check your foundation; these check whether you can grow people, run delivery predictably, and translate engineering into business outcomes.

On this page Team management Project management Release & delivery Metrics that matter Difficult situations Practice questions
📖 The story

Here’s the reframe that changes everything: your product is the team. Features are downstream of whether you’ve built a healthy, capable group that ships well without you in the room.

So the work flows in a sequence — hire and grow the people, give them a way of working that makes delivery predictable, then measure outcomes (not activity) and feed what you learn back into the team.

Every hard situation — the underperformer, the conflict, the missed date — is handled the same way: face it early, with candor and care, and fix the system that produced it, not just the symptom.

Team management

Your product is the team. Everything else is downstream of whether you've built a healthy, capable, motivated group of people. The recurring theme directors are tested on: do you scale yourself through others, or do you stay the hero?

Hire & build

Raise the bar with every hire, design the org so teams map to loosely coupled systems (Conway's Law), and create clear ladders so growth is visible. The best lever you have is who's on the team.

Grow & retain

Regular 1:1s focused on the person, not just status. Delegate real ownership (including the right to do it differently). Give feedback early and specifically. People leave managers, not companies.

Performance

Address underperformance directly and kindly — set clear expectations, give support and a timeframe, and don't let it linger; nothing erodes a strong team faster than tolerated underperformance.

Psychological safety

The strongest predictor of team performance (Google's Project Aristotle). People must feel safe to admit mistakes, ask questions, and challenge ideas. Blameless post-mortems are this principle in action.

Situational vs. servant leadership

Two frames worth naming. Situational leadership: adapt your style to the person and task — more direction for the new engineer, more delegation for the senior one. Servant leadership: your job is to remove obstacles and set people up to succeed, not to be the smartest person in the room. Most strong directors blend both.

Say it like a director

"I measure my own success by the team's output and growth without me in the room. If I'm the bottleneck or the hero, I've failed as a leader — my job is to build a group that consistently makes good decisions on its own, and to spend my energy on the few things only I can do."

Project management

You're not expected to be a certified PM, but you must run delivery predictably and talk fluently about how work flows. Know the models and, more importantly, when each fits.

ApproachBest whenWatch out for
Agile / ScrumRequirements evolve; you want fast feedback and incremental value.Cargo-cult ceremonies that add process without adding value.
KanbanContinuous flow, support/ops work, variable priorities.No WIP limits → everything in progress, nothing done.

The estimation trap

When asked "how do you make sure projects ship on time," don't promise certainty. Say: "Estimates are ranges, not promises. I reduce risk by breaking work down, shipping in thin vertical slices, tracking against forecast weekly, and surfacing slippage early rather than at the deadline. I manage scope and expectations as actively as I manage the work." Predictability comes from small batches and honest signals, not heroics.

The iron triangle

Release & delivery management

How code gets safely and repeatedly into customers' hands. Modern delivery is about making releases small, frequent, and boring — because small changes are easy to test, easy to roll back, and rarely cause big outages.

Deployment strategies

Blue-green: run two environments, switch traffic instantly, roll back instantly. Canary: release to a small % first, watch metrics, then ramp. Both shrink blast radius.

Feature flags

Decouple deploy from release. Ship code dark, turn it on for a cohort, kill it instantly if it misbehaves — no redeploy. Powerful, but flag debt is real; prune them.

Rollback & recovery

The question isn't "will it break" but "how fast can we recover." Fast, practiced rollback beats a perfect release process. This is the "time to restore" DORA metric.

Say it like a director

"I push teams toward small, frequent releases with automated pipelines, canary rollouts, and feature flags. The philosophy is that safety comes from a fast, reliable path to production and an even faster path to roll back — not from heavyweight approval gates. I'd rather we deploy fifty small changes a week that are each trivial to reverse than one big monthly release we all hold our breath through."

Metrics that matter

Lead with outcomes, support with delivery health, and never weaponize metrics against individuals. The gold standard for delivery is the four DORA metrics.

DORA metricWhat it tells you
Deployment frequencyHow often you ship. Higher = smaller batches, healthier flow.
Lead time for changesCommit → production time. Shorter = faster feedback and value.
Change failure rate% of deploys causing a problem. Lower = better quality gates.
Time to restore serviceHow fast you recover from failure. The resilience number.
Leading vs. lagging indicators

Lagging indicators tell you what already happened (revenue, churn, last quarter's incidents). Leading indicators predict the future (deploy frequency, test coverage, engagement scores). Good directors watch leading indicators so they can act before the lagging ones go bad.

Goodhart's Law

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Track velocity and you'll get inflated story points. Track lines of code and you'll get bloat. Use metrics to start conversations and spot trends — never as individual performance scorecards.

Difficult situations (have a frame ready)

  • Underperformer: diagnose (skill vs. will vs. fit), set clear expectations, support with a timeframe, document, and act decisively if it doesn't improve.
  • Two strong engineers in conflict: hear both privately, find the shared goal, focus on behavior and impact, and align on a decision — disagree and commit.
  • Missed deadline: communicate early, present options (cut scope, add time, add risk), own it without blame, and fix the systemic cause in the retro.
  • Pressure to cut corners: make the trade-off explicit and its risk visible to decision-makers; take on debt deliberately and write down the payback plan.
  • Attrition risk: understand the real driver (growth, comp, manager, burnout), act on what you can, and never be surprised — stay-interviews beat exit-interviews.

Practice questions

How do you measure the success of your engineering team?Core

I lead with business outcomes — did we move the metric the company cares about — then look at delivery health through the four DORA metrics: deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, and time to restore. I treat those as trend lines and conversation starters, not individual scorecards, because the moment a metric becomes a target it gets gamed. I also watch leading indicators of team health like engagement and on-call load, since burned-out teams produce bad lagging numbers a quarter later.

How do you ensure projects are delivered on time?Trap

I don't promise certainty — I engineer predictability. Break work into thin vertical slices that each ship value, forecast in ranges, and track against that forecast weekly so slippage surfaces early, not at the deadline. When something's at risk I bring options — cut scope, move the date, or accept defined risk — rather than ask the team to be heroes. And I manage expectations with stakeholders as actively as I manage the work itself. The trap is claiming you always hit dates; the credible answer is that you make reality visible early and adjust deliberately.

An engineer is underperforming. Walk me through how you handle it.Core

First I diagnose the cause, because the response differs: is it a skill gap, a motivation/will issue, a role-fit problem, or something personal going on? I have a direct, kind conversation setting out specific expectations and where they're falling short, then agree on support and a clear timeframe to improve. I document, I check in frequently, and I genuinely try to help them succeed. But if it doesn't improve, I act decisively — letting underperformance linger is unfair to the rest of the team and quietly lowers everyone's bar. The throughline is candor with care.

Two of your best engineers strongly disagree on a technical direction. What do you do?Deep

Disagreement among strong people is healthy — my job is to channel it. I'd hear each perspective, separate facts from preferences, and re-anchor everyone on the shared goal and the actual constraints. Often the "right" answer is "run a small spike and let data decide" if it's reversible. If it's a one-way door and they still disagree, I make the call, explain the reasoning, and ask for disagree-and-commit. What I won't do is let it fester or pick the louder voice — I want a culture where being overruled with a clear rationale is normal and safe.

How do you balance shipping features with paying down technical debt?Deep

I treat tech debt as a deliberate financial decision, not a moral failing. Some debt is smart leverage to hit a market window; the danger is unmanaged debt with no payback plan. Practically, I keep a steady allocation — often around 15–20% of capacity — for debt and reliability, make the cost of debt visible in business terms (slower delivery, more incidents), and tie paydown to the areas slowing us down most. Using error budgets helps: when reliability slips below target, we shift the balance toward fixing it. The framing for stakeholders is that debt is borrowing against future velocity, and I manage the interest.

How do you onboard into a new org and earn trust in the first 90 days?Core

Listen before I lead. The first month is mostly learning — 1:1s with my team, peers, and stakeholders; understanding the systems, the metrics, and where the pain is. I look for a credible early win that helps the team without disrupting what's working, so I build trust through delivery rather than announcements. By 60–90 days I'd share a point of view on priorities, co-created with the team, not imposed. The mistake I avoid is arriving with a reorg and a manifesto on day one — that signals ego over understanding.

How do you run effective 1:1s?Core

I treat 1:1s as the report’s meeting, not my status update — they own the agenda. I focus on them as a person: career growth, blockers, feedback in both directions, and how they’re really doing, not just task tracking (which can happen elsewhere). I hold them consistently and don’t cancel them, because reliability is what builds the trust that makes hard conversations possible later. I listen more than I talk, take notes, and follow through on what I commit to. Over time, the signal I watch for is whether they bring me real problems early — that means the relationship is working.

How do you set goals or OKRs for your org?Deep

I start from the company’s strategy and translate it into a few outcomes my org owns — outcomes, not output (‘reduce checkout latency to under X’, not ‘ship feature Y’). I involve the teams in shaping the key results so they have ownership rather than handed-down targets, keep the set small enough to focus, and make them measurable. I separate aspirational goals from committed ones so we’re honest about what’s a stretch. Then I review progress regularly and treat misses as learning, not punishment — if every objective is hit, they weren’t ambitious enough. I’m also wary of Goodhart: goals guide focus; they aren’t individual scorecards.

Two teams have a dependency conflict that’s blocking a launch. What do you do?Trap

First I’d get the facts and the shared goal in one room rather than letting it escalate over chat — what each team needs, the real deadline, and the customer impact of slipping. Most of these are prioritization or sequencing problems, not technical ones. I’d look for the unblock: can we stub the dependency, deliver a thin slice, or reprioritize a sprint? If the teams can’t resolve it, I make the call as the leader who can see across both, explain the reasoning, and own the trade-off. Then the systemic fix: surface why the dependency wasn’t visible earlier and improve cross-team planning so we see these before launch week.

How do you scale yourself as your org grows from one team to five?Deep

The shift is from managing work to managing managers and systems. I move from being in the details to setting clear direction, hiring and developing strong leaders, and building the mechanisms — planning rhythms, decision frameworks, metrics — that let teams make good calls without me. I deliberately let go of things I used to own and accept they’ll be done differently. I protect a small amount of my time for the highest-leverage, hardest-to-delegate work: strategy, cross-org unblocking, and senior hiring. The failure mode I watch for in myself is staying the bottleneck or the hero; my job is to make the org effective when I’m not in the room.

How do you build a culture of operational excellence?Deep

I make reliability a first-class, shared value rather than an afterthought. Concretely: blameless post-mortems so people surface problems instead of hiding them; clear SLOs and error budgets so reliability has a seat at the prioritization table; on-call that’s humane and owned by the teams who build the systems (you build it, you run it); and operational metrics reviewed as seriously as feature delivery. I celebrate prevention and good incident response, not just shipping. Culture follows what leaders pay attention to and reward, so I consistently spend attention on operational health — that’s what signals it actually matters.

🧠 Remember it

Your product is the team — everything else is downstream.

  • Measure success by what the team ships without you.
  • Predictability comes from small batches + honest early signals.
  • Address problems with candor + care; fix the system, not the symptom.
