What an Engineering Director actually owns

The single biggest mistake in director interviews is answering like a senior engineer — going deep on how to build something. Directors are accountable for outcomes across teams, not the implementation of any one of them. Your scope is people, systems, and business results, in that order of leverage.

DimensionEngineer thinksDirector thinks
Unit of workA task / a serviceA portfolio of teams & systems
Time horizonSprint to quarterQuarter to multi-year
Definition of doneIt works & is mergedIt's operated, measured, and creating value
Main leverWriting codeHiring, org design, prioritization, standards
Failure questionWhy did this break?Why did our system let this break, and how do we prevent the class of failure?
Say it like a director

"My job isn't to have the best answer in the room — it's to make sure the team consistently produces good answers without me. I optimize for the system that produces outcomes, not for any single outcome."

The trade-off mindset

Senior technical leaders almost never say "the best way is X." They say "it depends on what we're optimizing for." Every meaningful decision trades one good thing for another. Naming the trade-off out loud is the clearest signal of seniority you can give.

  • Speed vs. quality — ship fast and learn, or build to last? Usually answered by reversibility (below).
  • Build vs. buy — is this our core differentiator, or undifferentiated heavy lifting we should buy/rent?
  • Consistency vs. availability — the CAP trade-off that drives most data architecture decisions.
  • Cost vs. performance vs. reliability — you can usually have two cheaply; the third costs money.
  • Centralize vs. decentralize — shared platform efficiency vs. team autonomy and speed.
Reversible vs. one-way-door decisions

Borrowed from Amazon. Reversible (two-way door) decisions are cheap to undo — make them fast, delegate them, don't over-analyze. One-way-door decisions are expensive or impossible to reverse — slow down, gather data, involve more people. Knowing which is which is a director-level skill. Most decisions are reversible; treat them that way to move fast.

Say it like a director

"This is a reversible decision, so I'd rather we pick a reasonable option this week and learn from real usage than spend a month seeking the perfect one. If it were a one-way door — say, a data model our partners integrate against — I'd slow down and invest in getting it right."

Core technical concepts you must speak fluently

You won't be asked to implement these, but you must explain them crisply and know when each matters. Treat the cards below as flashcards — read the plain-language version until you can teach it.

Latency vs. throughput

Latency = how long one request takes. Throughput = how many requests per second you can handle. Optimizing one can hurt the other (batching boosts throughput but adds latency). Always ask "which one matters for this user experience?"

Concurrency vs. parallelism

Concurrency = dealing with many things at once (structure). Parallelism = doing many things at once (execution, needs multiple cores). A single-core machine can be concurrent but not parallel.

CAP theorem

In a distributed system facing a network Partition, you must choose between Consistency (every read sees the latest write) and Availability (every request gets a response). You can't fully have both during a partition. Most modern systems pick AP with "eventual consistency."

ACID vs. BASE

ACID (Atomic, Consistent, Isolated, Durable) — strong guarantees, classic relational DBs, money & orders. BASE (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent) — relaxed guarantees for scale & availability, common in NoSQL.

Synchronous vs. asynchronous

Sync = caller waits for the result (simple, tightly coupled). Async = fire and continue, result comes later via callback/queue/event (resilient, scalable, harder to reason about). Async + queues absorb spikes and decouple teams.

Statefulness

Stateless services hold no client data between requests — easy to scale horizontally and replace. Push state into databases, caches, and object stores. "Make the compute stateless" is a reflex you should have.

Coupling & cohesion

Aim for low coupling (components depend on each other minimally) and high cohesion (a component does one thing well). This is the heart of good architecture and good org design — teams should map to loosely coupled systems (Conway's Law).

Idempotency

An operation is idempotent if doing it twice has the same effect as doing it once. Critical for retries, payments, and at-least-once message delivery. "Make the API idempotent with a request key" is a senior reflex.

Say it like a director

"I'd push the team toward stateless services behind a load balancer, with state in a managed datastore. It keeps scaling boring — we add instances — and it keeps failure boring, because any instance can die without taking a user's session with it."

Metrics literacy — speak in numbers

Directors are expected to manage by evidence. You don't need to compute them, but you must know what they mean and reach for them naturally.

Trap to avoid

Don't quote "99.999% uptime" reflexively. Each extra nine costs roughly 10× more. A director asks "what reliability does this use case actually need, and what does the next nine cost us?" Matching reliability to business need is more impressive than chasing five nines everywhere.

Practice questions

As a director, how do you decide what to work on yourself vs. delegate?Core

I delegate by default and reserve my own time for the few things only I can do: setting direction, unblocking across orgs, hiring, and the highest-stakes one-way-door decisions. My test is "does this build the team's capability or just my own throughput?" If doing it myself makes me a bottleneck or robs someone of growth, I delegate it with clear context and let them own the outcome — including the right to do it differently than I would.

The pattern

State your default (delegate), name your exceptions (direction, hiring, cross-org unblocking, one-way doors), and give the test you apply.

What's the difference between latency and throughput, and why does it matter to a leader?Core

Latency is how long a single request takes; throughput is how many we can serve per second. They matter to me because they map to different user pains and different costs. A checkout flow lives and dies on p99 latency, so I'd invest there. A nightly batch job only cares about throughput, so I'd happily add latency to process more per run. The leadership move is making sure the team optimizes the dimension the business cares about, not the one that's most fun to optimize.

Explain CAP theorem to a non-technical executive.Deep

"Imagine our data lives in two data centers. Normally they stay in sync. But if the link between them breaks, we face a choice for that moment: either refuse some requests so nobody sees stale data — that's choosing consistency — or keep serving everyone, accepting that two users might briefly see slightly different data — that's choosing availability. We can't perfectly have both while the link is down. For a bank balance we'd lean toward consistency; for a social feed, availability. We make that choice deliberately, per use case."

Why this lands

It shows you can translate a technical theorem into a business trade-off an exec can decide on — the core director skill.

A team wants five-nines (99.999%) availability for an internal admin tool. How do you respond?Trap

I'd push back gently with a question, not a no: "What does an outage of this tool actually cost us, and who feels it?" Five-nines means roughly five minutes of downtime per year and typically multi-region redundancy, on-call rotations, and a large operational tax. For an internal admin tool used in business hours, three-nines is almost certainly the right target, freeing that engineering investment for customer-facing systems. The lesson I'd reinforce with the team: reliability is a cost we choose to pay where it earns its keep, not a vanity number.

When would you choose eventual consistency over strong consistency?Deep

When availability and scale matter more than every reader seeing the absolute latest value instantly — and when the business can tolerate a short convergence window. Think likes, view counts, feeds, product catalogs. I'd keep strong consistency where correctness is non-negotiable: money movement, inventory at the point of sale, anything where a stale read causes a wrong decision. The framing I'd give the team is: "What's the cost of someone reading data that's two seconds old?" If it's near zero, eventual consistency buys us a lot of resilience cheaply.

How do you make decisions when you don't have enough information?Core

First I classify the decision: is it reversible or a one-way door? If it's reversible, I'd rather make a good-enough call quickly and learn from reality than wait for certainty that may never come — speed is itself an advantage. If it's a one-way door, I invest in reducing uncertainty: time-boxed spikes, prototypes, talking to customers, or a small reversible experiment to de-risk the irreversible part. I also make my assumptions explicit so we can revisit the decision when they change.
